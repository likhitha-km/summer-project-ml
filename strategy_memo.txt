EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
─────────────────
We analyzed 140,859 trip segments across 1,590 facilities and
2,506 corridors that make up the network. On a majority of
shipments (84.8%), actual transit time deviates from the
system's baseline routing estimate (OSRM) by more than 20%. This is not a
reflection of SLA performance today, but it does mean our routing engine
under-represents real network behavior — and that gap is precisely where a
graph-based view of the network creates value: by learning how delays
actually propagate through hubs and corridors, we can produce ETAs that hold
up under real operating conditions.

MODEL IMPACT — FROM ROUTING ESTIMATE TO RELIABLE ETA
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Augmenting our ETA model with network structure (hub centrality, corridor
congestion patterns, and facility connectivity) improves prediction error
from 10.34 to 9.91 minutes (4.2% reduction) and lifts the
share of shipments with an ETA within 15% of actual delivery time from
36.9% to 41.1% (+4.2 points). In practical terms: more
shipments arrive within the window we promise customers, fewer last-minute
escalations, and capacity planning that reflects how the network truly
behaves — not just how it would behave on an empty road.

CORRIDOR-SPECIFIC INTERVENTIONS
────────────────────────────────
- Chronic corridors (transit time consistently >20% over OSRM): 1,853 corridors
  identified, accounting for 83,915 breach-trips in the
  training window.

- Recommended intervention by corridor profile:
  – Long haul (>300km) with high breach rates → shift to FTL (cost-neutral above 350km)
  – Urban last-mile, night operations → add a dedicated night shift at the source hub
  – Medium-distance corridors with seasonal spikes → pre-position surge capacity at top 3 hubs
